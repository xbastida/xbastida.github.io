Dbizi · San Sebastián

Redesigning the bike-sharing network

A data-driven model that finds where to place stations so more people can ride, fewer staff hours are spent rebalancing bikes, and fewer truck trips pollute the city.

The problem

Dbizi has 70 stations across the city. Many were placed years ago by intuition. Some are chronically empty at rush hour; others sit full all day and require constant rebalancing. With real usage data now available, we can do better.

This tool asks: given a budget of N stations, where should they go to maximise ridership while minimising operational burden?

Three things the model cares about

🚲
Demand

How much cycling activity a location is expected to attract. Predicted by a machine-learning model (ExtraTrees) trained on the 70 current stations, then applied citywide to 15,000 m² hexagonal cells. Each station captures demand from its surroundings — shaped by elevation, nearby bike-sharing network density, residential population, the concentration of points of interest such as workplaces, restaurants, and transit stops, among others.

⚖️
Rebalancing burden

Some locations structurally generate imbalance: gravity, commute patterns, and uneven land use create persistent flows that staff must reverse by truck every day. The model penalises these locations using LIS (Lost Imbalance Surplus — bike-hours outside a ±10 bike comfort band) scaled by day-to-day variability, so erratic stations pay an extra cost.

👥
Population coverage

Some parts of the city will never be high-demand corridors — and some are an operational headache to serve. But they still need access. Population coverage is a very present metric in the model: layouts that leave too many residents further than 300 m from a station are penalised, keeping equity visible throughout the optimisation.

What predicts demand?

The model was trained on actual Dbizi trip counts per station, normalised by effective catchment area. Eight neighbourhood-level features were tested; the strongest predictors:

Bike lane length nearby
Workers in the area
Restaurants, bars & shops within 300 m
Residential population
Land-use entropy (mix of uses)
Altitude (steeper = less use)
City-centre flag
Income per person

How the optimizer works

1
Score every hexagon

Each of the ~1 000 feasible city hexagons gets a demand score, a rebalancing cost, and a stability index — all normalised to [0, 1].

2
Greedy warm start

Stations with mandatory infrastructure (electrical hookups, intermodal nodes) are fixed. Remaining slots are filled greedily — always picking the hexagon with the best marginal gain.

3
Simulated annealing

300 000 random station swaps are proposed. Good swaps are always accepted; slightly bad ones are sometimes accepted too — this lets the algorithm escape local optima. Temperature cools gradually, converging to a stable solution. Three independent restarts are run; the best is kept.

4
Size each station

The total number of docks scales proportionally with the number of stations. Within that budget, stations with high effective demand, high imbalance, and high occupancy variability are assigned more docks (large); the rest get fewer.

What you can tune in the demo

N
Number of stations
How many stations to place. Current network has 70. More stations improve coverage and unlock latent demand, but increase capital cost.
W
Demand weight
W = 1 → pure ridership maximisation. W = 0 → pure rebalancing minimisation. Default 0.6 balances both.
λ
Proximity penalty
Discourages placing two stations too close together. Higher λ spreads stations out more aggressively.
POP
Coverage target
A penalty applied to layouts that fall short of the set coverage threshold — population within 300 m of a station. Raising this makes the optimizer penalise under-served residential areas more heavily.
